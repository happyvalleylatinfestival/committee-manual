Section 6

Vendor Management

Vendors are the heart of the festival experience — the food and crafts that make it feel like a real cultural celebration. This section covers how to recruit, select, and manage them.

Food Vendors offer authentic Latin American cuisine from diverse regional traditions. Priority goes to vendors who can show cultural authenticity in their food and preparation. They must provide current health department permits, food handler certifications, and a certificate of liability insurance naming Latin Vibe as additional insured.

Craft Vendors sell handmade items reflecting Latin American cultural traditions — jewelry, textiles, artwork, pottery, and similar goods. We prioritize makers over resellers, and vendors who can speak to the cultural significance of their work.

Launch the application in late June as a Google Form hosted on the festival website, and keep it open through mid-August. The form should collect: business name and contact info, type of products or food, description of cultural connection, pricing information, space and power requirements, and uploaded copies of required documents.

Promote the opening through social media, direct outreach to previous vendors, and community networks. All responses automatically collect in a Google Drive spreadsheet — share access with the Main Coordinator and Finance Coordinator from the start.

Applications close mid-August. Review for completeness first, then cultural fit and quality. Build a balanced mix considering: cuisine variety (avoid too much duplication), geographic representation across Latin America, price points that are accessible, and dietary options.

Give priority to vendors owned by members of Latin American or Latino communities, vendors with strong community connections, and vendors who participated successfully in previous years. Maintain a waitlist for cancellations. Send acceptance and rejection notices promptly so vendors have time to confirm.

  • Valid business license or vendor registration
  • Certificate of general liability insurance naming Latin Vibe as additional insured
  • Current health department permits and food handler certifications (food vendors only)
  • Detailed menu or product list with pricing
  • Booth setup specifications including dimensions and power requirements
  • Photos of booth setup and products (helps with space planning)

Load-in typically starts 4–6 hours before opening. Assign time slots to prevent congestion. Be present the entire time to direct vendors and resolve placement issues.

During the festival, stay available to handle vendor concerns, monitor compliance, and escalate any serious issues to the Main Coordinator.

Load-out: supervise breakdown, confirm proper waste disposal, and make sure every space is left clean. Collect immediate feedback from vendors — it's invaluable for the debrief.

Section 7

Information Tables

Information tables connect festival attendees with Latino-serving organizations, community resources, and educational programs. They're free or low-cost, non-commercial, and mission-aligned.

Priority goes to organizations serving Latino and Hispanic communities, cultural and educational organizations aligned with the festival mission, community service organizations with relevant programming, local government services and public health organizations, academic institutions with Latino studies programs, and nonprofits offering resources to immigrant communities.

Political organizations and primarily commercial entities generally don't qualify — though evaluate case by case. A bank offering free Spanish-language financial literacy workshops could be appropriate; purely promotional materials would not.

Applications open at the same time as vendor applications — late June — and close in mid-August. Applications are submitted via Google Form. Organizations must describe their mission and how they connect to Latino communities.

Accepted organizations must provide their own table, two chairs, covering and materials, staff their table the full festival, bring materials in English and Spanish when possible, limit sales activities (small fundraising items may be approved in advance), and follow all festival guidelines.

Send each accepted organization a packet including: assigned table location, load-in time and instructions, parking information, the day-of contact number, and reminders about staffing and material requirements.

Section 8

Artists & Performances

The performance lineup is the festival's centerpiece. This section covers everything from finding artists to getting them through sound check on the day.

Research artists representing diverse Latin genres: salsa, bachata, cumbia, regional Mexican, Latin jazz, folkloric, reggaeton, contemporary Latin pop. Prioritize performers with festival experience who represent diverse Latin American countries.

Sources: previous festival performers, community member recommendations, regional booking agencies specializing in Latin music, university Latin music programs, and local musicians with strong community connections. Unlike vendors, artist recruitment usually happens through direct outreach, not open applications.

Request from each artist: press photos, audio/video samples, their technical rider, and references from previous performances.

All bookings must be in writing. Contracts should specify: performance date, time, and set length; agreed compensation and payment schedule; technical requirements; cancellation policy; promotional obligations; and liability requirements. All contracts must be reviewed by the Main Coordinator before signing.

A typical festival day has 4–6 performance slots ranging from 30–60 minutes each. Design for energy: open with an upbeat, accessible act, vary genres and intensity throughout, and schedule the most anticipated act at a strategic peak time. Build in buffer time between sets for sound checks and transitions.

Share the draft schedule with the Main Coordinator and Marketing Coordinator before finalizing. Then distribute to all artists with their specific arrival and sound check times.

Arrive early to greet artists and walk them through the space. Provide each artist a welcome packet with the full day schedule, their specific performance time, parking info, coordinator contact numbers, and restroom locations.

Stay near the stage throughout the festival. Coordinate with the Main Coordinator on payments — most are processed per contract, either before or immediately after performance. Have all payment documentation ready, including completed W-9 forms.

Section 9

Sponsors

Sponsorships provide crucial funding and demonstrate community support. Strong sponsor relationships often grow over multiple years, so every interaction reflects on Latin Vibe's reputation.

  • Presenting Sponsor ($5,000–$10,000+): Logo on all materials, naming rights, large banner, social media features, speaking opportunity at opening, VIP hospitality access, press recognition
  • Major Sponsor ($2,500–$5,000): Logo on materials and website, banner display, social media recognition, press release inclusion, stage acknowledgment
  • Supporting Sponsor ($1,000–$2,500): Logo on website and select materials, name recognition at festival, social media mentions
  • Community Sponsor ($500–$1,000): Name on website, listing in printed materials, social media acknowledgment

Coordinate with the Marketing Coordinator to confirm promised benefits are actually deliverable. Document all commitments clearly in the sponsorship agreement.

Target: local businesses with Latino ownership or significant Latino customer base, companies with demonstrated commitment to diversity, businesses near the festival venue, financial institutions, healthcare providers, educational institutions, and corporations with community giving programs.

Prioritize sponsors who have supported the festival or Latin Vibe before — those relationships are easier to maintain and often grow over time.

Create a tracking sheet in Google Drive to monitor: sponsor level and amount committed, payment status, which benefits have been delivered, and any special requirements. Check off each deliverable as it's completed.

Within two weeks after the festival, send each sponsor a thank-you package including: appreciation note, festival success highlights, photos showing their branding, and a save-the-date for next year. Keep sponsors engaged throughout the year — invite them to other Latin Vibe events and share updates about the organization's work.

Section 10

Volunteers

Volunteers are the hands that make the festival run. The Volunteer Coordinator is responsible for making sure every station is staffed, every person is trained, and no one is left wondering what to do on festival day.

Open volunteer sign-ups via Google Form on the festival website. Promote through social media, the Latin Vibe newsletter, Penn State student organizations, local community groups, and direct asks to past volunteers.

Collect: name, contact info, availability on festival day, areas of interest or past experience, and whether they speak Spanish or Portuguese.

Common volunteer stations include: vendor load-in/load-out support, information and welcome tables, stage area assistance, artist hospitality, information table area support, parking and accessibility assistance, cleanup crews, and roving support. Work with each area coordinator 4–6 weeks out to confirm how many volunteers they need and for which time slots.

Hold at least one orientation session in the 2–4 weeks before the festival. Cover: the festival schedule and layout, each volunteer's specific role and station, what to do if something goes wrong, who to call for help (Volunteer Coordinator's phone number), and where the key facilities are (restrooms, first aid, food).

Send a reminder email 2–3 days before with their shift time, where to check in, and what to wear.

Set up a volunteer check-in station near the festival entrance. As volunteers arrive, confirm their assignment, give them any materials they need (vest, badge, printed schedule), and direct them to their station.

Stay mobile throughout the day. Check in on each station. Be the person coordinators call when they need a volunteer gap filled. At the end of the day, collect any festival materials from volunteers and thank them personally — it's what brings people back next year.

Section 11

Year Honoree

Each year, the festival recognizes someone who has made a meaningful contribution to the Latin community in the Happy Valley region. The 2026 Year Honoree is Sandra del Pilar.

The selection process should be clearly defined each year and documented here. At minimum, it should include a nomination period where committee members or community members can suggest candidates, defined criteria for what makes a strong honoree (community impact, leadership, cultural contribution), and a committee review to reach consensus.

Once selected, the honoree should be notified personally and well in advance — give them time to prepare remarks, invite guests, and be recognized properly.

Coordinate with the Artist Coordinator to schedule the recognition within the performance program — typically between sets to maintain crowd energy. Make sure the ceremony is accessible: confirm the stage area is reachable for anyone with mobility needs, and that a microphone is available.

Prepare a brief biographical statement to be read aloud. Provide a physical keepsake for the honoree (plaque, certificate, or similar). Document the recognition with photos or video for the YouTube channel and social media.

Section 12

Website Management

The festival website is hosted on WordPress via ReclaimHosting. It's the home base for all applications — vendor, artist, volunteer, and information table. Keeping it accurate and functional is critical throughout the planning cycle.

  • Updating Google Forms for all application types each year
  • Publishing and updating the performance schedule when finalized
  • Keeping vendor, artist, and info table information current
  • Adding sponsor logos and recognition as directed by Sponsor Relations Coordinator
  • Posting announcements and important updates throughout planning
  • Maintaining the committee manual site (this site)
  • Troubleshooting form and website issues quickly

The festival website runs on WordPress via ReclaimHosting. All application forms are built and managed in Google Forms, with responses flowing into Google Drive spreadsheets. Access credentials for both the WordPress admin panel and the Google Drive folder should be stored securely and shared only with the Website Manager and Main Coordinator.

This committee manual site is hosted on GitHub Pages. Contact the Main Coordinator for access to the repository.

  • Review all Google Forms from the previous year — update dates, contacts, and requirements
  • Test form submission and confirm responses are flowing into Google Drive correctly
  • Update any outdated festival information on the website (dates, location, contacts)
  • Confirm that access credentials still work and are up to date
  • Check that the performance archive from the previous year is saved and accessible
Section 13

Social Media & Marketing

The festival's social media presence runs on Facebook and Instagram, in English, Spanish, and sometimes Portuguese. Campaigns start at least three months before the festival. The Marketing Coordinator depends on every other coordinator for content.

Build a content calendar that covers the full run-up to the festival. Types of posts to plan for: application announcements (vendor, artist, volunteer, info table), artist spotlight posts once lineup is confirmed, vendor previews in the weeks before, sponsor recognition posts (required per sponsorship agreements), event reminders and countdown posts, and day-of and recap posts.

All promotional content should be available in English and Spanish at minimum. Build in extra time to get translations reviewed by a fluent speaker before posting.

  • Vendor Coordinator: Names, descriptions, and photos of accepted vendors (for vendor spotlight posts)
  • Artist Coordinator: Artist names, bios, photos, and performance times as soon as they're confirmed
  • Sponsor Relations: Sponsor logos and any specific recognition language required by agreements
  • Information Tables: Names of participating organizations
  • Volunteer Coordinator: Volunteer recruitment post copy and deadline information

Build a shared Google Drive folder where coordinators can drop materials. Set clear deadlines so you're not chasing people at the last minute.

The Marketing Coordinator also manages print materials including: festival posters and flyers, yard signs and sponsor banners, the performance lineup poster, and any Town & Gown or TV ad placements. Work with a local printer on specifications and turnaround times — budget at least 3 weeks for design, approval, and printing before materials need to be distributed.

Sponsor yard signs and banners must match the dimensions and placement specifications agreed to in sponsorship contracts. Confirm details with the Sponsor Relations Coordinator before going to print.

Section 14

Print Materials

Print materials are coordinated by the Marketing Coordinator but involve input from nearly every area. Plan ahead — print takes time and last-minute changes are expensive.

  • Posters and flyers: General festival promotion. Distributed to local businesses, Penn State, community centers, and libraries. Designed for English and Spanish versions.
  • Lineup poster: Performance schedule with all artist names and set times. Finalized as late as possible to be accurate, but must be to print at least 2–3 weeks before the festival.
  • Sponsor yard signs and banners: Must match contracted dimensions exactly. Confirm specs with Sponsor Relations before any design work starts.
  • Town & Gown magazine ads: Check deadlines well in advance — magazine print deadlines are typically 4–6 weeks before publication.

Allow roughly this much time for each stage: design (1 week), internal review and approval (3–5 days), revision if needed (2–3 days), printing (1–2 weeks depending on quantity and complexity), distribution (1 week). Start the process earlier than you think you need to — something always takes longer than expected.

Section 15

Grants

Grant funding supports the festival's costs and Latin Vibe's broader programming. Start grant research early — many funders have deadlines 6 or more months before funds are distributed.

The Happy Valley Adventure Bureau (HVAB) marketing grant deadline falls in early April — well before the committee formally meets in June. This means the Grants Coordinator (or Main Coordinator if the role isn't filled yet) must handle this application independently, months before general planning begins.

Confirm the exact deadline each year as soon as possible. Required materials typically include: a festival description, projected attendance figures, a marketing plan summary, and the previous year's budget or actuals. If Latin Vibe is awarded the grant, a post-event report is due in December — calendar that deadline at the time of award.

  • Sheetz: Community giving program — research the current application cycle and deadlines each year. Deadline may fall before or after June.
  • Pennsylvania Council on the Arts: Supports cultural organizations and events. Check eligibility and lead times — some cycles require applications 6+ months in advance.
  • Local community foundations, corporate giving programs from State College–area employers, and other regional funders.

Maintain a running list in Google Drive with each funder's deadline, eligibility requirements, typical award amount, and application status. Share access with the Main Coordinator and Finance Coordinator.

Most applications ask for some combination of: a description of Latin Vibe and its mission, a description of the festival and its community impact, a detailed project budget, attendance and demographic data from previous years, and letters of support if required. Keep a shared Google Drive folder with these core materials updated each year so applications are faster to write.

The HVAB post-event report is due in December. Don't wait until then to gather materials — collect attendance numbers, photos, and budget actuals during the October/November debrief phase while everything is fresh. Report writing in December is much faster when the materials are already organized.

A typical post-event report includes: a narrative of what happened, financial accounting of how grant funds were used, final attendance numbers, marketing materials produced (save copies of posters, social posts, ads), and photos from the festival. Submit on time and save a copy in Google Drive — it also becomes useful source material for next year's application.

Section 16

Finances

The Main Festival Coordinator holds access to Latin Vibe's bank account and processes all payments. The Finance Coordinator supports by tracking the budget, monitoring expenses, and producing reports.

Start with the previous year's actual expenses as your baseline. Build the budget in a Google Sheet shared with the Main Coordinator. Key expense categories include: artist fees, sound and stage equipment rental, permits and insurance, vendor management costs, print and marketing materials, street decoration materials, volunteer and hospitality expenses, and administrative costs.

Revenue sources include: vendor booth fees, sponsorships, grant awards, and any ticket sales if applicable. Build a small contingency buffer (10–15% of total expenses) for unexpected costs.

Maintain a running expense tracker in the Google Sheet. Every coordinator should inform the Finance Coordinator before making any purchases. All receipts go to the Main Coordinator for reimbursement or payment processing. Nothing is reimbursed without a receipt.

Flag any area that's trending over budget to the Main Coordinator as early as possible — small overruns caught early are much easier to manage than large ones found at the end.

Within 2–4 weeks after the festival, finalize all expense tracking, collect any remaining vendor fee payments, and produce a final financial summary comparing budgeted versus actual figures for each category. This summary is shared with Latin Vibe's board and used as the starting point for next year's budget planning.

Section 17

Street Decorating

The Fraser Street block transforms in the days before the festival. This coordinator plans the visual look of the space and leads the setup crew, working closely with the Volunteer Coordinator.

Develop a decoration plan for the festival footprint — MLK Jr. Plaza and the 100 Block of Fraser Street. The decoration should reflect Latin American visual traditions and create an inviting, festive atmosphere. Common elements include: papel picado banners, flags from Latin American countries, colorful streamers and fabric, flower arrangements, and signage.

Confirm the plan with the Main Coordinator well in advance. Check whether the Borough has any restrictions on the type of decorations or how they may be attached to structures or fixtures in the public space.

  • Papel picado banners or flags
  • Hanging string lights (if permitted)
  • Fabric or streamers in festival colors
  • Country flags or cultural symbols
  • Adhesive or attachment materials that won't damage surfaces
  • Ladders and tools for hanging (confirm volunteers are comfortable with heights)
  • Festival signage and directional signs
  • Sponsor banner stands or mounts

Coordinate with the Volunteer Coordinator to schedule a setup crew for the day(s) before the festival. Produce a simple diagram of where each decoration element goes so volunteers can work independently. Bring printed copies of the plan on setup day.

After setup, do a walkthrough to make sure everything is secure, nothing blocks vendor load-in or emergency vehicle access, and the space looks cohesive. Make any adjustments before the festival opens.

Section 18

Post-Festival Debrief

The debrief is how this manual stays useful. Every coordinator attends, every area is reviewed, and every lesson learned gets documented so next year's team doesn't start from scratch.

Schedule the debrief within 2 weeks of the festival, while memories are fresh. Each coordinator should come prepared to answer three questions about their area: What worked well and should be kept? What was harder than expected and why? What would you change for next year?

The Main Coordinator facilitates. Designate a note-taker who captures every action item with a name and a deadline. The goal is a written summary that can inform next year's planning from day one.

After the meeting, circulate the notes to all coordinators for review and additions. Then update the relevant sections of this manual with any changes to procedures, contact info, timelines, or requirements. If a role's time commitment was consistently wrong, update the estimate in the Roles section.

Save the debrief notes in the shared Google Drive folder under the relevant year. Future coordinators can reference them when planning.

The Finance Coordinator and Main Coordinator finalize all budget actuals. Compare budgeted vs. actual for every category and note where significant differences occurred. This comparison is the starting point for next year's budget.

The Grants Coordinator files any required post-event reports within their deadlines. Collect attendance numbers, photos, and financial documentation before they're hard to find.
